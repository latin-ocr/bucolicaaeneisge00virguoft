THIS eclogue represents (after Theocritus, Idyl iv. and v.) the rivalry in
song of two shepherds, Menalcas and Damoetas. After some dispute—
and a dash of blackguard ——the decision is left to Palaemon as umpire, and
the two rival swains vie in alternate couplets. This form of verse is called
Amoebaean (ἀμοιβαῖος, respon-vive). The couplets are wholly disconnected,
some of them mere squibs flung out, it is supposed, by the poet at his
rivals. Though the Amoebaean verse is Greek, and the poem itself copied
from Theocritus, yet the alternate abuse is thoroughly Italian. The Romans
were very fond of coarse invective and repartee, and these form the staple
of the Salurcz (one of the earliest forms of Latin drama), the Fescennine
and Atellane farces, and the Mimes. (Cf. Teuffel, Gesclziclzle der R'o'mi-
