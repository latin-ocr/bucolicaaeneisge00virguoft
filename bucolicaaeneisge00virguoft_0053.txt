ECLOGUE VIII.

THIS poem—imitated from Theocritus—consists of two parts, sung in
rivalry by the shepherds Damon and Alphesiboeus, whom the poet repre-
sents himself to have overheard. The first part tells the sorrows of a
shepherd at the loss of his love (Idyl iii.). The last tells the magle arts
employed by a maiden skilled in sorcery to win back the love of the incon-
stant Daphnis (Idyl ii.) : hence the title sometimes given to it of Pharma-
ceulrz'a, “The Sorceress.” It is supposed to have been written in B.C. 39,
and is inscribed to Pollio, whose military exploits are alluded to in vs. 6—13.
