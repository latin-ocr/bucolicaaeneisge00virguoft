ECLOGUE II.

THE subject of this poem is the complaint of a shepherch Corydon, in
love with a boy Alexis. It is said to represent the admiration of Vix-gil
sor a young slave whom he saw at the house of his patron Asinius Pollio,
and whose beauty he thus celebrates, in the couventional style of pastoral
verse. The story further goes that Pollio, charmed with the poem, made
a gift of the slave to the author3 and that the slave, being carefully edu-
cated, became a celebrated grammarian under his real name Alexander.
This story, though not certain, is natural and probable. Some parts of
the poem are imitations of Theocritus (Idyls iii., xi.).
